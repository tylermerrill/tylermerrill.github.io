Information Reporting and Backup Withholding

          A person making payments of tax-exempt interest to a bondholder is generally required to make an
information report of the payments to the Internal Revenue Service and to perform “backup withholding” from the
interest if the bondholder does not provide an IRS Form W-9 to the payor. “Backup withholding” means that the
payor withholds tax from the interest payments at the backup withholding rate, currently 24%. Form W-9 states the
bondholder’s taxpayer identification number or basis of exemption from backup withholding.

         If a holder purchasing a Bond through a brokerage account has executed a Form W-9 in connection with the
account, as generally can be expected, there should be no backup withholding from the interest on the Bond.

          If backup withholding occurs, it does not affect the excludability of the interest on the Bonds from gross
income for Federal income tax purposes. Any amounts withheld pursuant to backup withholding would be allowed
as a refund or a credit against the owner’s Federal income tax once the required information is furnished to the Internal
Revenue Service.

The Project

         The facilities being developed by the Company will be a biofuels production facility, which will convert
approximately 1,070 tons per day (or 375,000 tons per year) of solid waste (predominantly waste fats, oils, greases
and other agricultural oils) into approximately 6,500 barrels per day (“BPD”) (or approximately 90,000,000 gallons
per year (“MGPY”)) of renewable diesel fuel and other co-products. The treatment and disposal of solid waste is
through a refining process without discharging the solid waste. The facilities consist of buildings, structures,
machinery, equipment, utilities, and support systems and components and property that is functionally related and
subordinate to the foregoing, to be acquired, constructed, improved, and installed at an approximately 30-acre site
located at the GT OmniPort, 1998 H O Mills Blvd., Port Arthur, Texas, within the boundaries of the Issuer (the
“Project Site”). See “APPENDIX A — THE COMPANY, MANAGEMENT AND PROJECT OVERVIEW.”

Information Reporting and Backup Withholding

          A person making payments of tax-exempt interest to a bondholder is generally required to make an
information report of the payments to the Internal Revenue Service and to perform “backup withholding” from the
interest if the bondholder does not provide an IRS Form W-9 to the payor. “Backup withholding” means that the
payor withholds tax from the interest payments at the backup withholding rate, currently 24%. Form W-9 states the
bondholder’s taxpayer identification number or basis of exemption from backup withholding.

         If a holder purchasing a Bond through a brokerage account has executed a Form W-9 in connection with
the account, as generally can be expected, there should be no backup withholding from the interest on the Bond.

income for Federal income tax purposes. Any amounts withheld pursuant to backup withholding would be allowed

                      THE COMPANY, MANAGEMENT AND PROJECT OVERVIEW

The Company

         Emerald Renewable Diesel LLC, a Delaware limited liability company and registered to do business in
Texas (the “Company”), is a single purpose entity formed in October 2018 to develop, construct, install, equip,
commission, own and operate a biofuels production facility to be located in Port Arthur, Texas, which upon
completion and start-up will convert solid waste (predominantly waste fats, oils, greases and other agricultural oils)
into renewable diesel fuel and other co-products (the “Project”).

        The sole member of the Company is Emerald Investco LLC, a Delaware limited liability company a single
purpose entity formed in October 2018 to directly or indirectly raise and contribute equity capital to the Company
(the “Investment Company”). The members of the Investment Company will be Emerald Biofuels LLC, a
Delaware limited liability company (the “Sponsor”), and one or more equity investors.

         The Sponsor has been primarily responsible for the development and design of the Project, sourcing the
feedstocks, identifying the offtake partner, entering into the material agreements, and engaging various professionals
and advisors to obtain the financing.

The Market

         The U.S. renewable fuel market was created by federal and state government mandated initiatives,
including the Energy Policy Act of 2005, as amended, which created Renewable Fuels Standard (“RFS”) and
renewable identification numbers (“RINs”), and state mandates, most notably in California, which created the Low
Carbon Fuel Standard (“LCFS”) and a Cap-and-Trade program with the goal of reducing 2030 greenhouse gas
(“GHG”) emission levels to 40% below those in 1990. These governmental programs generally require that
renewable fuels replace or reduce the amount of petroleum-based transportation fuel, heating oil and/or jet fuel
through the blending of renewable fuels with gasoline or diesel fuel by refiners or importers or the purchase and use
of renewable fuels. Refiners or importers generally may demonstrate compliance by obtaining tradable credits such
as RINs and LCFS credits.

         To satisfy these goals and earn the applicable credits, renewable diesel is emerging as a significant fuel in
the global transportation industry. Renewable diesel has properties of conventional diesel and can be used in
vehicles, trucks and other transporters that use diesel fuel without blending with petroleum diesel, but is produced
from 100% renewable sources and contains no fossil carbon.

          Worldwide production of renewable diesel has increased from about 300 million gallons in 2011 to about
1.8 billion gallons in 2016, a 500% demand increase in 5 years. As of today, there are only four renewable diesel
refineries currently operating in the United States, which are inadequate to meet this growing domestic and
worldwide demand.

The Project

         Upon completion, the Project is expected to be a state-of-the-art, sustainable renewable diesel refinery that
will produce commercial grade ultra-low sulfur diesel fuel, ASTM D975, and certain co-products, primarily naphtha
and LPG, from renewable feedstocks such as waste oils, fats, greases and other agricultural oils. At projected
nameplate production capacity, the Project is expected to convert approximately 1,070 tons per day (or 375,000 tons
per year) of waste fats, oils and greases and other agricultural oils into approximately 6,500 barrels per day (“BPD”)
(or approximately 90,000,000 gallons per year (“MGPY”)) of renewable diesel fuel and other co-products.

         The Company currently anticipates the Project to be completed by December 31, 2021 and commence
operations by January 31, 2022.

    The Project Site

          The Project will be located on the GT OmniPort site, an 1100-acre industrial site located 85 miles east of
Houston, on Taylor Bayou adjacent to Port Arthur, Texas. The Company believes that the Project site’s strategic
location along the Gulf Coast allows for convenient and cost-effective transportation options for both feedstock and
renewable diesel, and that the existing infrastructure at the Project site will minimize capital investment and reduce
the risks that would be associated with greenfield development.

Feedstocks

          Biomass based diesel (“BBD”), as defined by the US EPA in its implementation of Renewable Fuel
Standard 2 (“RFS2”), includes both renewable diesel and biodiesel. Both are produced by converting glyceride-
based feedstocks, but result in completely different products. Renewable diesel is a hydrocarbon, while biodiesel is
a methyl ester. BBD is made from glycerides, which are fatty acid-based molecules that store excess energy in the
form of oils or fats. In general, oils are liquid at room temperatures, while fats are solid. BBD feedstocks are split
into: (i) purpose-grown agricultural crops, such as soybean and canola, and (ii) second-use materials, known
collectively as fats, oils and greases (“FOGs”), such as yellow grease, beef tallow and distillers corn oil.

          The use of the UOP EcofiningTM hydrotreating technology allows the Project to be feedstock flexible and
process about fifteen tested and approved glyceride feedstocks with no adverse effect on the quality of the BBD
product. As described below, this flexibility will not only allow the Project to use lower cost available feedstock but
also to control the most important characteristic of renewable diesel – its cold flow properties, that is, the
temperature at which the fuel starts to solidify (i.e., “jell up” in the fuel tank and fuel line) and fail to flow to the
engine causing the engine to stop. This is a major concern with the use of biodiesel. Other significant attributes that
differentiate renewable diesel from biodiesel are:

             •    The use of biodiesel is restricted as a chemical additive, whereas renewable diesel is a “drop-in”
                  fuel that can be used with no restrictions, including existing diesel engines without modification;

             •    Biodiesel has lower energy content;

             •    Biodiesel is restricted in the range of feedstocks that can be used to produce it;

             •    Biodiesel has highly limiting cold flow properties; and

             •    Biodiesel inherits the cold flow attributes of its feedstock, meaning that, generally, lower quality
                  feedstocks produce lower quality biodiesel.

         The ability to use multiple feedstocks and to blend the mix of renewable feedstocks (subject to limitations
on the feedstock mix mandated by the Internal Revenue Code and the regulations thereunder) allow the Project to
realize lower production costs. The Project will require approximately 103 MGPY of feedstock at nameplate
capacity and 124 MGPY at expected operating capacity.

                      THE COMPANY, MANAGEMENT AND PROJECT OVERVIEW

The Company

         Emerald Renewable Diesel LLC, a Delaware limited liability company and registered to do business in Texas
(the “Company”), is a single purpose entity formed in October 2018 to develop, construct, install, equip, commission,
own and operate a renewable diesel refinery in Port Arthur, Texas, that will produce commercial grade ultra-low sulfur
diesel fuel to the standard of ASTM D975, and certain Co-Products (as defined below), from renewable feedstocks
such as waste oils, fats, greases and other agricultural oils (the “Project”).

          The sole member of the Company is Emerald Investco LLC, a Delaware limited liability company a single
purpose entity formed in October 2018 to directly or indirectly raise and contribute equity capital to the Company (the
“Investment Company”). The members of the Investment Company will be Emerald Biofuels LLC, a Delaware
limited liability company (the “Sponsor”), and one or more equity investors.

         The Sponsor has been primarily responsible for the development and design of the Project, sourcing the
feedstocks, identifying the offtake partner, entering into the material agreements and engaging various professionals
and advisors to obtain the financing.

The Market

          The U.S. renewable fuel market was created by federal and state government mandated initiatives, including
the Energy Policy Act of 2005, as amended, which created Renewable Fuels Standard (“RFS”) and renewable
identification numbers (“RINs”), and state mandates, most notably in California, which created the Low Carbon Fuel
Standard (“LCFS”) and a Cap-and-Trade program with the goal of reducing 2030 greenhouse gas (“GHG”) emission
levels to 40% below those in 1990 through reducing the carbon footprint of the fuel use, particularly fuel used for
transportation. These governmental programs generally require that renewable fuels replace or reduce the amount of
petroleum-based transportation fuel, heating oil and/or jet fuel through the blending of renewable fuels with gasoline
or diesel fuel by refiners or importers or the purchase and use of renewable fuels.

        The LCFS further established a credit trading mechanism for regulated parties to demonstrate their
compliance with the LCFS requirements. Refiners and importers of petroleum products can generate LCFS credits
by purchasing and importing biofuels outside of California, purchasing biofuels inside California, and blending into
petroleum fuel.

         To satisfy these goals and earn the applicable credits, renewable diesel is emerging as a significant fuel in
the global transportation industry. Renewable diesel has the same properties of petroleum-based diesel and can be
used in vehicles, trucks and other transporters that use diesel fuel without blending with petroleum diesel, but is
produced from 100% renewable sources and contains no fossil carbon.

          Worldwide production of renewable diesel has increased from about 300 million gallons in 2011 to about 1.8
billion gallons in 2016, a 500% demand increase in 5 years. As of today, there are only four renewable diesel refineries
currently operating in the United States, which are inadequate to meet this growing domestic and worldwide demand.

Feedstocks

          Biomass based diesel (“BBD”), as defined by the US EPA in its implementation of Renewable Fuel Standard
2 (“RFS2”), includes both renewable diesel and biodiesel. Both are produced by converting glyceride-based
feedstocks, but result in completely different products. Renewable diesel is a hydrocarbon, while biodiesel is a methyl
ester. BBD is made from glycerides, which are fatty acid-based molecules that store excess energy in the form of oils
or fats. In general, oils are liquid at room temperatures, while fats are solid. BBD feedstocks are split into: (i) purpose-
grown agricultural crops, such as soybean and canola, and (ii) second-use materials, known collectively as fats, oils
and greases (“FOGs”), such as yellow grease, beef tallow and distillers corn oil.

          The use of the UOP EcofiningTM hydrotreating technology allows the Project to be feedstock flexible and
process about fifteen tested and approved glyceride feedstocks with no adverse effect on the quality of the BBD
product. As described below, this flexibility will not only allow the Project to use lower cost available feedstock but
also to control the most important characteristic of renewable diesel—its cold flow properties, that is, the temperature
at which the fuel starts to solidify (i.e., “jell up” in the fuel tank and fuel line) and fail to flow to the engine causing
the engine to stop. This is a major concern with the use of biodiesel. Other significant attributes that differentiate
renewable diesel from biodiesel are:

              •    The use of biodiesel is restricted as a chemical additive, whereas renewable diesel is a “drop-in”
                   fuel that can be used with no restrictions, including existing diesel engines without modification;

              •    Biodiesel has lower energy content;

              •    Biodiesel is restricted in the range of feedstocks that can be used to produce it;

              •    Biodiesel has highly limiting cold flow properties; and

              •    Biodiesel inherits the cold flow attributes of its feedstock, meaning that, generally, lower quality
                   feedstocks produce lower quality biodiesel.

         The ability to use multiple feedstocks and to blend the mix of renewable feedstocks (subject to limitations on
the feedstock mix mandated by the Internal Revenue Code and the regulations thereunder) allow the Project to realize
lower production costs. The Project will require approximately 103 MGPY of feedstock at nameplate capacity and
124 MGPY at expected operating capacity.

Federal and State Benefits

          Enhancing the Project’s financial projections are renewable diesel production benefits from the federal
government-mandated use of biofuels in the U.S. transportation industry such as RINs and the FBTC. Furthermore,

Trade incentives create significant value of as much as $1 per gallon. Under the ExxonMobil Agreement, such federal
and state benefits are transferred to ExxonMobil upon its purchase of the renewable diesel product, which is built into
the price per gallon.

Project Feasibility

          The Independent Engineer has conducted a thorough review of the Project and concluded that (i) the Project
is capable of producing renewable diesel fuel suitable for use as an economically attractive alternative to purchased
market renewable diesel fuel in transportation applications, (ii) the quality of the renewable diesel fuel and Co-

(iii) similar projects are proven and in operation in at Diamond Green Diesel in Norco, LA, at a scale that is similar to
the Project and (iv) the Company’s refining process has significantly lower environmental impacts compared to
conventional refining facilities and overcomes the problems and limitations previously encountered in the production
of renewable diesel fuel.
